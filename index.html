Tour Sites

Muhammad Ali Center

Opened in 2005 to promote cross-cultural understanding and empowerment.

Charles Anderson Jr. Marker

Commemorates the career of the first African American elected to a southern state legislature.

Simmons University/Louisville Municipal College

Kentucky's first African-American-controlled institution of higher learning

Old Central High School

Kentucky's first public school for black students (established as Central Colored High School in 1873).

Freedom Park at UofL

Features ten markers depicting the story of black progress from settlement to the present, plus biographies of past and present UL civil rights activists.

Open Accommodations Mass Protests

Site of a sit-in launched by a group of black teenagers in February 1961 to protest discriminatory treatment at downtown restaurants and retail stores.

Pendennis Club

This private club was notorious for excluding blacks, jews, and women until finally adopting an open policy in 2006.

Old Walnut St. Business District and Mae Street Kidd

Louisville's historic black business district, including Mammoth Life and Accident Insurance Co. Mammoth Employee Mae Street Kid served in the state House of Representatives (1968 - 1984) where she co-introduced the state's fair housing bill.

Zion Baptist Chuch and Reverend A.D. King

Martin Luther King Jr.'s younger brother became minister in 1965 and established a Kentucky chapter of the Southern Christian Leadership Conference.

I. Willis Cole Home

Memphis native I. Willis Cole founded the Louisville Leader, a prominent civil-rights-oriented black newspaper, and the I. Willis Cole Publishing Company.

Lyman Johnson Home

After challenging the state's Day Law, 43-year-old educator Lyman Johnson became the first African-American student to enroll in graduate school at the University of Kentucky.

Braden Memorial Center

Longtime home of the Kentucky Alliance Against Racist and Political Repression and hub of anti-raist activism.

Reverend Louis Coleman Jr. Drive

Louis Coleman, Jr., longtime pastor at First Congregational Church, was one of the city's most tireless civil rights activists from the 1970s until his death in 2008.

Fontaine Ferry Park

Site of the popular amusement park open from 1905 through 1969 (whites only until 1964).

Chickasaw Park

One only two Louisville parks open to black people until 1955.

Braden Home

Home of social justice activists Carl and Anne Braden.

Muhammad Ali Boyhood Home

The boxing legend lived at 3302 Grand Avenue with his parents, Odessa and Cassius Sr, and brother, Rudolph.

1968 Civil Disorder

An uprising that resulted in two deaths, hundreds of arrests, and millions of dollars in property damage began with demonstrations at this site.

A.D. Porter & Sons Funeral Home

Founded in 1907, one of the oldest African-American-owned businesses in Louisville.

Western Branch Library

One of the first libraries in the nation to cater to black patrons, and now home to Louisville's African American Archives.

Quinn Chapel A.M.E. Church

The original location of the church that's been prominent in civil rights activism since 1870, when protesters gathered to end segregated seating in streetcars.

Open Housing Pioneers--The Wade Home

When the Wade family integrated this neighborhood in 1954, they were subjected to intimidation and harassment. They were forced to leave for good when their home was dynamited.
